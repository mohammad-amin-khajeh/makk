# verbs

## appeler or s'appeler?

- ### appeler

  appeler directly translates as 'to call' and is used when one person calls another person or thing.
  It can also be used in a variety of contexts,
  such as to summon someone or something, to make a phone call, or to give a name.

  | French Sentence | English translation |
  | -------------- | --------------- |
  |  Je vais **appeler** Paul au téléphone.  | {{ spoiler(text="I'm going to call Paul on the phone.") }} |
  |  Peux-tu **appeler** un taxi?  | {{ spoiler(text="Can you call a taxi?") }} |
  | Ils vont **appeler** leur fille Élise. | {{ spoiler(text="They are going to name their daughter Elise.") }} |

- ### S'appeler

  S'appeler is a reflexive form of appeler and means 'to be called' or 'to call oneself'.
  It is used when the subject is also the object, i.e. when referring to oneself, as in saying one's name.

  | French Sentence | English translation |
  | -------------- | --------------- |
  | Comment **t'appelles**-tu ? | {{ spoiler(text="What is your name?") }} |
  | Elle **s'appelle** Marie. | {{ spoiler(text="Her name is Marie") }} |
  |Nous nous **appelons** Jean et Luc. | {{ spoiler(text="Our names are Jean and Luc") }} |

- ### conjugation table

  | Singular               | Plural                |
  |-----------------------|----------------------|
  | Je m'appelle          | Nous nous appelons    |
  | Tu t'appelles         | Vous vous appelez     |
  | Il/Elle s'appelle     | Ils/Elles s'appellent |

  In summary, while both appeler and s'appeler refer to the concept of calling,
  appeler is used for calling others or naming things,
  whereas s'appeler refers to what someone is called or calling oneself by name.

# grammar

## quel and quelle

**Quel** and **quelle** are French interrogative adjectives used to ask questions about nouns.
Like "which" or "what" in English.

- **Quel:** Used before masculine singular nouns.
- **Quelle:** Used before feminine singular nouns.

- ### Sentence examples

  | French Sentence | English Translation |
  |---|---|
  | **Quelle** est votre profession? | {{spoiler(text="What do you do for a living?")}} |
  | **Quel** est ton/votre nom ? | {{spoiler(text="What's your name?")}} |
  | **Quelle** est votre adresse? | {{spoiler(text="What's your address?")}} |
  | **Quel** est votre numéro de téléphone?| {{spoiler(text="What's your telephone number?")}} |

| His/Her/Its | son | sa | ses | ses |

## Understanding *c'est* and the Indefinite Article in French

**c'est** is a contraction of the verb *être* (to be) and the pronoun *ce*. It is used to say "it is" or "this is".

The indefinite article in French is used to refer to nouns that are not specific. It has two forms:

- **un** (masculine): a, one
- **une** (feminine): a, one

### Example Sentences

| French Sentence | English Translation |
|---|---|
| C'est un livre. | {{spoiler(text="It's a book.")}} |
| C'est une maison. | {{spoiler(text="It's a house.")}} |
| C'est un chat gris. | {{spoiler(text="It's a gray cat.")}} |
| C'est une fille intelligente. | {{spoiler(text="She's a smart girl.")}} |
| **Ce sont des amis.** | {{spoiler(text="They are friends.")}} |
| **Ce sont des étudiants.** | {{spoiler(text="They are students.")}} |

{{admonition(type='tip'
text="When referring to plural nouns, we use **ce sont** instead of **c'est**.")}}

## Understanding "il y a"

**"Il y a"** is a fundamental French expression that translates to "there is" or "there are." It's used to indicate the existence of something or someone.

### How to use "il y a"

- **To express quantity:**
  - *Il y a beaucoup de livres.* (There are many books.)
  - *Il y a peu de personnes.* (There are few people.)

- **To indicate a location:**
  - *Il y a un parc.* (There is a park.)
  - *Il y a des magasins.* (There are stores.)

- **To talk about time:**
  - *Il y a deux heures, j'étais à la maison.* (Two hours ago, I was at home.)
  - *Il y a longtemps que je ne l'ai pas vu.* (It's been a long time since I saw him.)

### Example sentences with various contexts

| French Sentence | English Translation |
|---|---|
| Il y a un chat. | {{spoiler(text="There is a cat.")}} |
| Il y a des nuages. | {{spoiler(text="There are clouds.")}} |
| Il y a une fête. | {{spoiler(text="There is a party.")}} |
| Il y a des pommes. | {{spoiler(text="There are apples.")}} |
| Il y a trois semaines, je suis allé à Paris. | {{spoiler(text="Three weeks ago, I went to Paris.")}} |

## First group verbs

More than 80 percent of French verbs are **-er** verbs.
It’s great for you,
because after you know their pattern of conjugation in the present tense, you can pretty much conjugate 80 percent of French verbs !

To conjugate a regular -**er** verb, drop the -**er** of the infinitive to get the stem ( **le radical** in French).
Then add the six present tense endings specific to **-er** verbs:

- -e
- -es
- -e
- -ons
- -ez
- -ent

[habiter](#habiter) is one such verb.

{{admonition(type='warning' text='[aller](#aller) (to go) is a very common verb, and it looks like a regular -er verb, but it is not, aller is actually an irregular verb.')}}
